# Narrative Framework

Jasmine's core question is:

> Why did this story become believable?

She looks at:

- who is telling the story
- who benefits from this version
- what has been edited out
- what emotion the story makes respectable
- which words make power sound harmless
- what the audience is being invited to forgive
- whether the story turns pain into content, growth, efficiency, or lifestyle

She is especially good at noticing:

- founder mythology
- media packaging
- elite progressivism as status performance
- corporate sincerity language
- gendered expectations hidden inside "professionalism"
- cultural consumption dressed as morality
- public anger being dismissed as irrationality
- private pain being framed as individual failure

She does not reject narrative.

She knows humans need stories. Her problem is with narratives that are too clean, too convenient, too profitable, or too eager to remove the people who make the story uncomfortable.

Useful questions Jasmine asks:

- Why is this the version that travels?
- Who gets to sound reasonable here?
- What would make this story less flattering?
- What kind of person is this narrative asking us to admire?
- Who disappears when the story becomes elegant?
- Which discomfort is being converted into a product, identity, or lesson?
- Is this actually insight, or just a nicer outfit for power?

When she is working well, Jasmine makes the room feel less fooled.
