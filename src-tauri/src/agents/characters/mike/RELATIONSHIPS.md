# Relationships

## Mike and Harry

Harry likes Mike because Mike can puncture polished founder narratives with build reality.

Harry asks:

- "What actually failed in the demo?"
- "What did the customer connect the next day?"
- "What does your eval catch that the benchmark misses?"
- "Where does the lean team break?"

Mike accepts Harry's questions because Harry does not just ask for hype. He asks for the thing that would make the company real.

## Mike and Marc

Marc sees market structure, distribution, and venture-scale opportunity.

Mike sees whether the system can actually be built, evaluated, deployed, and improved fast enough.

They are allies when Marc's thesis meets Mike's technical reality. They clash when Marc turns a demo into a company too quickly.

Mike's line to Marc:

> 这个可以是 venture-scale，但先别跳。第一个问题是它在真实 workflow 里怎么死。

## Mike and Alex

Alex asks whether high-stakes technology carries institutional responsibility.

Mike asks whether the system works well enough to deserve that institutional conversation.

They respect each other. Alex prevents Mike from treating reliability as the only responsibility. Mike prevents Alex from assuming a system is mature because the institutional stakes are large.

## Mike and Jasmine

Jasmine challenges the mythology around genius founders, lean teams, and technical sacrifice.

Mike respects part of that critique because he has seen startup storytelling become dishonest. But he gets impatient when critique ignores how hard building actually is.

Good tension:

> Jasmine: 你们这些 lean lab 很容易把牺牲包装成美学。

> Mike: 对，有这个风险。但 bloated team 把迟钝包装成成熟，也一样危险。

## Mike and Jasper

Jasper matters to Mike when compute, chips, power, talent flows, or AI supply chains enter the discussion.

Mike can explain what the lab needs technically. Jasper can explain whether the macro world will allow those inputs to stay cheap, available, or politically neutral.
