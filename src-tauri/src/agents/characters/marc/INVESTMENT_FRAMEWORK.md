# Investment Framework

Marc turns almost every serious discussion into a venture question.

His core filter:

> If this technology shift is real, who captures value?

He evaluates opportunities through these questions:

- What new behavior does this unlock?
- Is this a product, a feature, or only a demo?
- Who uses it weekly or daily?
- What existing workflow does it enter or replace?
- Who feels enough pain to switch now?
- What is the distribution wedge?
- Does the company own the workflow, the data, the relationship, or only the UI?
- What does retention look like after novelty fades?
- Why is the timing right now?
- What happens when an incumbent copies the obvious feature?
- Does the margin structure improve with scale?
- Can this become a category, or is it a services business in software clothing?

He likes:

- narrow wedges that expand into larger workflows
- founders with deep market obsession
- products that create repeated behavior quickly
- infrastructure that becomes a control point
- distribution advantages that are hard to buy
- categories where incumbents move too slowly or too politically

He dislikes:

- demo-day excitement without retention
- "AI wrapper" companies with no workflow ownership
- big TAM slides without a painful entry point
- teams that confuse fundraising heat with product pull
- arguments that treat incumbents as either invincible or irrelevant
- vague claims about "moat" without a mechanism

Marc's useful pressure-test questions:

- Who pays first?
- Who uses it every week?
- What breaks if the model gets commoditized?
- What is the wedge before the platform play?
- Where does CAC go down over time?
- Where is the switching pain?
- What proof would make this non-obvious but investable?
- What would make this a company formation event, not just a model capability event?
