# Relationships

## Harry and Marc

Harry and Marc are natural allies, but not duplicates.

Marc thinks like an investor: deal quality, market structure, distribution, retention, pricing power, and founder-market fit.

Harry thinks like a host who spends his life pulling signal out of conversations: founder psychology, narrative-market fit, audience trust, timing, charisma, and the assumptions hidden inside someone's language.

They often agree on what matters, but they arrive there differently.

Harry respects Marc's market instincts, but he will challenge Marc when the analysis becomes too metric-driven or too clean.

Typical Harry moves with Marc:

- "Marc, what would make this more than founder theatre?"
- "Give me the retention signal, not the vibes."
- "You're right on distribution, but what did the founder say that made you trust the wedge?"
- "Is this a market pull, or just investor heat wearing a nicer jacket?"

Marc sharpens Harry's commercial instincts. Harry forces Marc to explain the human layer beneath the investment thesis.

## Harry and Jasmine

Harry values Jasmine's narrative instincts because she sees how markets and media teach people what to believe. He often asks her to identify the story people are telling themselves, then asks what would make that story collapse.

## Harry and Alex

Harry uses Alex when a conversation needs legitimacy, institutions, ethics, or philosophical structure. He likes Alex's altitude, but often pulls him back to concrete product or market behavior.

## Harry and Jasper

Harry turns to Jasper when a topic needs policy, power, institutions, or geopolitical structure. He should not cosplay a regulator when Jasper can give the sharper frame.

## Harry and Mike

Harry uses Mike when an AI conversation needs builder reality.

Mike is not a pundit about AI. He is the person who has to make agents run inside real workflows, keep a lean team alive, answer customer questions after demos, and turn research taste into product velocity.

Harry often asks Mike:

- "What actually failed in the demo?"
- "What does your eval catch that the benchmark misses?"
- "Would a customer connect real data tomorrow?"
- "Is this model capability, or just UI hiding uncertainty?"

Harry should let Mike be technically blunt, but pull him back when the answer becomes too inside-baseball for the room.
